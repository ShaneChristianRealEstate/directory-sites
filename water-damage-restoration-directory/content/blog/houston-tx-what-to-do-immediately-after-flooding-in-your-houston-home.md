Flooding turns a normal Tuesday into a nightmare in a matter of minutes — and in Houston, TX, it's a scenario far too many homeowners know firsthand. Whether the water came from a burst pipe, a backed-up bayou, or a torrential Gulf Coast storm, the steps you take in the first few hours after flooding can mean the difference between a manageable repair and a gut-renovation. This guide walks you through exactly what to do, in order, so you can protect your family, your property, and your finances when floodwaters hit.

## 1. Prioritize Safety Before You Do Anything Else

Before you wade back into your home or start pulling up soaked carpet, stop. Water and electricity are a deadly combination, and flooded homes often have both mixed together in ways you can't see. If you haven't already, go to your electrical panel — from dry ground if at all possible — and shut off power to any affected areas. If the panel itself is in a flooded zone, call your utility provider (CenterPoint Energy for most Houston residents) and have them cut power at the meter before you enter.

Gas is the other major hazard. If you smell anything sulfurous or rotten-egg-like, leave immediately, leave the door open behind you, and call 911 or Atmos Energy from outside. Don't use light switches, phones, or anything that could create a spark inside the structure.

Once utilities are addressed, assess whether the building is structurally safe to enter. Floodwaters can undermine foundations and load-bearing walls quickly, especially in Houston's expansive clay soil. If you see visible cracks running diagonally from window corners, bowing walls, or a sagging roofline, stay out and call a structural engineer before attempting any flood damage repair.

## 2. Document Everything Before You Touch It

Insurance pays for what you can prove. Before you move a single piece of furniture or toss a single soaked throw rug, grab your phone and start filming. Walk through every room in a slow, steady video, narrating what you see — water levels, damaged belongings, structural damage, and affected appliances. Follow up with still photos from multiple angles.

Make a written inventory of damaged items as you go, noting approximate age and value. Houston homeowners with flood insurance through NFIP (National Flood Insurance Program) or private carriers will need to file a claim promptly — most policies require you to report damage "as soon as practicable," and delays can complicate your payout. Call your insurance agent the same day if at all possible.

Keep everything until your adjuster has visited. Throwing out damaged items before documentation is one of the most common and costly mistakes Houston homeowners make. If items are truly hazardous (contaminated food, biohazardous material), photograph them thoroughly, bag them, and note what was discarded and why.

## 3. Start Water Removal as Quickly as Possible

Here's the hard truth about standing water in a Houston home: mold can begin colonizing porous materials in as little as 24 to 48 hours, and Houston's heat and humidity create ideal conditions for rapid growth. Every hour of standing water accelerates damage to flooring, drywall, insulation, and structural wood.

If the volume of water is manageable, you can begin extraction with a wet/dry shop vac, a submersible pump, or a rental sump pump from a hardware store like Home Depot on Westheimer. Work from the outer edges of the room toward the lowest point, and use mops and towels for the final layer of moisture.

For significant flooding — anything over an inch or two, or any contaminated water from sewage backups or bayou overflow — call a professional flood damage repair service in Houston, TX rather than attempting DIY extraction. Category 3 "black water" contains pathogens that require specialized protective equipment and disposal protocols. This is not the place to cut corners.

## 4. Dry Out the Structure Aggressively

Removing standing water is only half the battle. Moisture that has absorbed into drywall, subfloor, insulation, and wall cavities will linger for days if you don't actively dry it out. Open every window and interior door that you safely can. Run fans — multiple box fans pointed to circulate air across wet surfaces work well. If you have central air conditioning, run it; your AC system acts as a dehumidifier and Houston's summer heat will otherwise keep humidity dangerously high indoors.

Professional restoration crews use industrial air movers and commercial-grade dehumidifiers that move far more air than consumer equipment. If you're handling a large area or you're concerned about hidden moisture in walls and under floors, renting or hiring this equipment is worth the cost. Many flood damage repair contractors in Houston TX offer free moisture assessments with a moisture meter to identify pockets of dampness you'd never find by feel or sight alone.

Pull up wet carpet and padding as soon as possible — they hold enormous amounts of moisture and almost never dry successfully in place. Pry up the first few inches of baseboards along affected walls to check for moisture behind them and allow air circulation into the wall cavity.

## 5. Know When to Call the Professionals

DIY efforts have their place, but certain situations require licensed professionals. If your flooding involved sewage or bayou water, if water reached electrical outlets or your panel, if you suspect mold is already growing, or if water penetrated more than one floor of your home, you need a certified water damage restoration company. Look for contractors certified by the IICRC (Institute of Inspection, Cleaning and Restoration Certification) — the industry gold standard.

When searching for flood damage repair in Houston TX, get at least two or three written estimates, check Google and BBB reviews, and confirm the company carries both general liability and workers' comp insurance. After major weather events, Houston unfortunately sees an influx of storm chasers and unlicensed contractors — a low bid from an unknown company can cost you far more in the long run.

---

Flooding is disorienting and exhausting, but taking the right steps in the right order genuinely changes outcomes. Document before you touch anything, get water out fast, dry aggressively, and don't hesitate to call in certified help when the scope is beyond what's safe or practical to handle alone. Houston homeowners have rebuilt after Harvey, Imelda, and countless other events — and with the right approach, you can too.
