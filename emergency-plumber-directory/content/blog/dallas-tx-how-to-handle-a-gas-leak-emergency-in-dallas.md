## Recognizing the Warning Signs of a Gas Leak

The most well-known sign of a natural gas leak is the distinctive rotten egg or sulfur smell — that odor is actually an additive called mercaptan, intentionally mixed into odorless natural gas by utility providers like Atmos Energy so you can detect a problem. If you notice that smell anywhere in your home, even faintly, treat it as a real emergency rather than waiting to see if it gets stronger.

Beyond the smell, pay attention to other physical clues. A hissing or whistling sound near a gas appliance, pipe, or meter is a serious red flag. You might also notice dead or discolored patches of grass over an underground gas line in your yard, bubbling water near buried pipes after rain, or an unexplained spike in your monthly gas bill. Inside your home, watch for pilot lights that keep going out or flames that burn orange or yellow instead of clean blue.

Physical symptoms matter too. If anyone in your household is experiencing unexplained headaches, dizziness, nausea, or fatigue — especially if symptoms improve when you go outside — carbon monoxide or a gas leak may be the culprit. Don't dismiss these signs as seasonal allergies or stress.

## The First 5 Minutes: What to Do Immediately

Speed and caution are both essential in those first few minutes. **Do not** turn any electrical switches on or off, including lights, ceiling fans, or appliances — a single spark can ignite accumulated gas. Leave phones and electronics alone until you're outside. Don't try to find the source of the leak yourself, and absolutely do not light matches or candles to "investigate."

Your priority is getting everyone out of the building fast. Grab people and pets, leave doors open as you go to allow ventilation, and move well away from the structure — at least 300 feet is a reasonable target. Once you're safely outside and away, then use your cell phone to call **911** and **Atmos Energy's 24-hour emergency line at 1-866-322-8667**. Atmos serves most of Dallas proper, though if you're in a suburb with a different provider, save that number in your phone now before you ever need it.

Do not re-enter the building for any reason until emergency responders have cleared it as safe. It's tempting to go back for documents, medications, or valuables — but nothing inside is worth the risk.

## Shutting Off Your Gas Supply Safely

If you can safely reach your gas meter without entering the building — it's typically located outside on the side or rear of the home — you can turn off the main gas supply using an adjustable wrench or a dedicated gas shutoff tool. The shutoff valve is the rectangular tab on the pipe leading into the meter; turn it perpendicular to the pipe to cut the flow.

Here's an important note: **only shut off the gas if you can do so without going back inside, and only if you feel confident doing it safely.** Many Dallas homeowners don't know where their meter is located until an emergency happens — take 10 minutes this weekend to find yours and make sure your wrench is accessible. Newer homes often have individual shutoff valves near each appliance as well, which is useful for isolated leaks.

Once the gas is off, leave it off. Even after the immediate danger passes, only a licensed plumber or your gas utility should turn the service back on. Restarting gas appliances incorrectly can create new hazards, including reigniting a leak or causing a backdraft in appliances with standing pilot lights.

## Preventing Gas Leaks Before They Happen

The best emergency is one that never occurs. Schedule an annual inspection of your gas lines, connections, and appliances with a licensed plumber. This is especially important in Dallas's older neighborhoods like Deep Ellum, Bishop Arts, and East Dallas, where homes may have original iron gas pipes that are decades old and prone to corrosion.

Install natural gas detectors on every floor of your home, similar to how you'd place smoke detectors. Combination carbon monoxide and gas detectors are widely available for under $50 and can alert you to a problem before it becomes dangerous. Test them monthly and replace batteries annually.

Finally, be mindful of any excavation work in your yard — buried gas lines are shallower than most homeowners assume. Before digging for landscaping, fencing, or irrigation, call **811 (the national "Call Before You Dig" line)** to have underground utilities marked. It's free, it's required by Texas law, and it takes just a few days of advance planning.
