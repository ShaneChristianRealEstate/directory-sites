## Recognizing the Warning Signs of a Gas Leak

The most obvious sign of a gas leak is smell — natural gas is treated with mercaptan, a chemical that gives it that distinct rotten egg or sulfur odor. If you catch even a faint whiff of it inside your home, take it seriously. Don't assume it's the drains or something you cooked. That smell is a deliberate warning system, and your instincts are right to be alarmed.

Beyond the smell, watch for other physical clues. You might notice a hissing or whistling sound near a gas line, appliance, or meter — even if the appliance isn't running. Dead or discolored vegetation in a specific patch of your yard can indicate an underground leak. Inside, look for bubbling or blowing dirt near buried pipes, unexplained condensation on windows near gas appliances, or a flame that burns orange or yellow instead of the healthy blue it should be.

Physical symptoms matter too. If multiple family members are experiencing simultaneous headaches, dizziness, nausea, or unusual fatigue — especially when indoors — carbon monoxide or a gas leak may be the culprit. Don't second-guess a situation where several of these signs are present at once.

## What to Do Immediately: The First Five Minutes

Speed and calm are your two best tools. The moment you suspect a gas leak, stop what you're doing and **do not turn any electrical switches on or off** — this includes lights, fans, garage door openers, and even your phone if it's nearby. A single spark from an electrical switch can ignite accumulated gas. Leave the lights as they are, whether on or off.

Get everyone out of the house immediately, including pets. Don't pause to grab valuables, turn off appliances, or investigate the source of the smell yourself. Leave the front door open as you exit to allow ventilation, but don't waste time — move quickly and deliberately. Once outside, move at least 300 feet away from the structure.

From a safe distance — ideally a neighbor's home or your cell phone well away from the property — call 911 first, then CPS Energy's emergency line at **1-800-870-1760** (CPS Energy serves the San Antonio area). Do not re-enter your home for any reason until emergency responders have inspected the property and given you the all-clear.

## What NOT to Do During a Gas Emergency

Homeowners sometimes make well-meaning mistakes that escalate danger dramatically. Never try to locate or repair a gas leak yourself — the risk of igniting accumulated gas is extreme, and gas line work in Texas requires a licensed professional. Even experienced DIYers should stay completely hands-off until the emergency is resolved.

Avoid using anything that could create a spark or flame: lighters, matches, candles, and cigarettes are obvious, but also avoid using your car if it's in an attached garage. The ignition system in your vehicle can spark. Similarly, don't use a landline phone inside the home — even the ringer can create a small electrical arc.

One common mistake is trying to turn off the gas at the meter yourself. Unless you've been trained to do this and have the right tool (a wrench), you could inadvertently make things worse. Leave the meter shutoff to CPS Energy technicians or a licensed gas leak plumber in San Antonio, TX who arrives after the immediate danger is assessed.

## After the All-Clear: Why You Still Need a Licensed Plumber

Once emergency responders have confirmed that gas levels are safe and the immediate danger has passed, your job isn't finished. CPS Energy will typically shut off gas service to your home and tag the line — they won't re-light appliances or diagnose the root cause of the leak. That's where a licensed gas leak plumber in San Antonio, TX comes in.

A qualified plumber will perform a pressure test on your gas lines to confirm the integrity of the entire system — not just the obvious failure point. They'll use professional leak detection equipment to identify any additional weak spots that might not have triggered the original emergency. This is especially important in older San Antonio homes with aging iron or corroded brass fittings.

Don't skip this step to save money. Many homeowners call for re-ignition of their appliances without a professional inspection and unknowingly live with a slow, low-level leak. Request written documentation of the inspection and any repairs made — this protects you from liability and may be required by your homeowner's insurance policy.

## Staying Safe Long-Term: Prevention Tips for San Antonio Homeowners

Schedule an annual gas line inspection with a licensed plumber — think of it like a yearly physical for your home's infrastructure. This is especially important if your home was built before 1980, when older galvanized steel and cast-iron piping was standard. San Antonio's clay-heavy soil can shift seasonally, putting stress on buried lines.

Install natural gas detectors on every floor of your home, particularly near sleeping areas and appliances. Combination CO/gas detectors are widely available and affordable. Test them monthly and replace batteries annually. Make sure every adult in your household knows the emergency shut-off procedure and the location of your gas meter.

Finally, keep your gas appliances professionally serviced. Water heaters, furnaces, and gas ranges should be inspected and cleaned regularly — a poorly maintained appliance is one of the most common sources of gas leaks in residential San Antonio homes.
