## What Counts as a Plumbing Emergency?

Not every plumbing issue requires an emergency call — and understanding the difference can save you the premium cost of after-hours service. True emergencies are situations where waiting until business hours would cause significant property damage, health hazards, or complete loss of water service.

In San Antonio, common genuine emergencies include burst pipes (especially during rare but brutal winter freezes like the 2021 Uri event), sewage backups that make toilets and drains unusable, gas line leaks near water heaters, and major water heater failures that leave you without hot water indefinitely. A slow-draining sink or a running toilet, while annoying, can typically wait until morning.

If you're unsure, err on the side of caution with anything involving active water flow you can't shut off, visible sewage exposure, or any smell of gas. In those situations, don't hesitate — shut off your main water valve, call a licensed emergency plumber in San Antonio, and if gas is involved, call CPS Energy's emergency line immediately.

## How to Vet an Emergency Plumber Before You Call

The single most important thing you can do — even with two inches of water on your floor — is take 90 seconds to verify a plumber's credentials. In Texas, all plumbers must be licensed through the **Texas State Board of Plumbing Examiners (TSBPE)**. You can verify any plumber's license in under a minute at the TSBPE website using their name or company name.

Beyond licensing, look for plumbers who carry **general liability insurance and workers' compensation coverage**. If an unlicensed or uninsured plumber causes further damage to your home, you could be financially responsible. Any reputable emergency plumber in San Antonio, TX should be able to recite their license number to you over the phone without hesitation.

Also check Google Reviews, the Better Business Bureau (BBB), and Yelp for recent reviews — specifically looking for comments about emergency response times, honest pricing, and whether the technician showed up when promised. A company with 200+ reviews and a consistent 4.5-star rating is a much safer bet than a brand-new listing with three glowing reviews.

## Questions to Ask When You Call

Once you've identified two or three candidates, make a quick call before committing. The right questions reveal a lot about how a company operates under pressure.

**Ask these upfront:**
- Are you licensed with the TSBPE? What's your license number?
- What is your service call or dispatch fee for after-hours emergencies?
- Do you provide a written estimate before any work begins?
- How soon can a technician arrive at my address in [your San Antonio neighborhood]?

Beware of any company that refuses to give a ballpark estimate over the phone, pressures you to commit immediately, or quotes a price that sounds impossibly low. Emergency plumbing in San Antonio typically runs **$150–$300 for a service call**, with total repair costs varying widely by job. Extreme lowball quotes are often a tactic used to get in the door before piling on hidden fees.

A trustworthy plumber will give you a written or clearly stated estimate before touching a single pipe, and will explain what the work involves in plain language.

## Understanding San Antonio-Specific Plumbing Challenges

San Antonio's environment creates some unique plumbing headaches worth knowing about. The city's water supply is notoriously **hard**, with high mineral content from the Edwards Aquifer. Over time, this causes calcium buildup in pipes, water heaters, and fixtures — meaning clogs and water heater failures are more common here than in softer-water cities.

San Antonio also sits on **expansive clay soil** that shifts significantly with wet and dry cycles. This ground movement is a leading cause of slab leaks — pressurized pipe leaks beneath your home's concrete foundation. If you notice warm spots on your floor, unexplained spikes in your water bill, or the sound of running water when everything is off, call an emergency plumber in San Antonio, TX who specifically has slab leak detection experience.

Finally, neighborhoods with homes built before 1980 — including large swaths of Alamo Heights, Southside, and older North Side developments — may have galvanized steel or cast iron pipes nearing the end of their lifespan. If your home is older, it's worth asking any plumber you hire about the overall condition of your pipes while they're already on-site.
